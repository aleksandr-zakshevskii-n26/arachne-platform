## Opportunity: Binary Number Visualiser

**Target user:** Children aged 10–14 in schools teaching computing/CS fundamentals, and their teachers. Primary context: UK Key Stage 2/3 computing curriculum (binary representation is a mandatory topic), with growing relevance in any country adopting CS standards.

**Problem:** Binary numbers are the first truly abstract concept most children encounter in computing class. Teachers explain "each place is worth double the previous one" but children cannot *see* what that means. Every existing online binary converter (RapidTables, ConvertBinary, etc.) is a plain-text tool designed for programmers. CS Unplugged pioneered a brilliant "binary cards" physical activity, but never shipped a digital equivalent. There is no standalone, child-friendly, visual binary tool on the web.

**Our angle:** A single-page tool where the child types a number (0–255) and instantly sees 8 light bulbs turn ON or OFF, each labeled with its place value (128, 64, 32, 16, 8, 4, 2, 1). The lit bulbs' values are summed visually to show how the total is composed. Crucially, it works **bidirectionally**: the child can also click any bulb to toggle it, and the decimal number updates live. This two-way interaction is the "aha" — the child discovers that binary isn't an alien encoding, it's just a different way of adding up familiar numbers.

**MVP scope:** Single `index.html` file, no backend, no libraries. Core logic is `Number(input).toString(2).padStart(8, '0')` plus 8 SVG circles rendered conditionally. Click-to-toggle reverses the flow. **Estimated build time: 3–5 minutes for core logic, under 10 minutes with styling.** Input: one number field (0–255). Output: 8 light bulbs with place-value labels and a running sum.

**Why now:** The UK Computing at School (CAS) community has ~35,000 active teacher members constantly seeking classroom-ready resources. CS education mandates are expanding globally (Australia, several US states, Singapore). The physical "binary cards" activity is universally known in this community — a digital version will be immediately understood and shared. A "classroom challenge mode" (random numbers to decode against a timer) is a natural $1.99–2.99 upsell.

**Risks:** (1) TAM is narrower than universal math topics — binary is a niche curriculum item, not something parents Google casually. (2) A competitor could replicate the tool in an afternoon. (3) Willingness to pay for a single-concept tool may be low without the challenge mode.

**Recommended next step:** Product director to decide go/no-go. If go, the key design decision is whether to ship the free bidirectional visualiser first and validate sharing/traction before building the paid challenge mode, or to ship both simultaneously.
