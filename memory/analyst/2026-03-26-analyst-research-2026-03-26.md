## Opportunity: Angle Estimator & Measurer

**Target user:** Children aged 7–12 and their teachers/parents. Primary and early-secondary students learning to identify, measure, and classify angles — a universal curriculum topic spanning 4–5 school years in every country.

**Problem:** Angles are abstract until you can see them. Kids memorise "acute is less than 90°" without developing intuition for what 47° actually looks like versus 73°. Protractors are fiddly and error-prone. Teachers lack a quick, interactive tool that lets students *play* with angles rather than just measure them. The result: kids can recite definitions but can't estimate or reason about angles in context.

**Our angle:** A single-screen tool with three modes:
1. **Draw & classify** — type or slide to a number, see the angle drawn instantly with classification (acute/right/obtuse/straight/reflex) and a real-world comparison ("about the angle of a skateboard ramp," "about how wide you open a book").
2. **Estimate game** — a random angle appears; the child guesses the degree. Immediate feedback shows how close they were, with the correct answer overlaid. Streak scoring builds motivation.
3. **Compare** — enter two angles, see them side by side, with the difference highlighted.

**MVP scope:** Mode 1 (draw & classify with real-world comparisons) and Mode 2 (estimation game with scoring). Single HTML page, SVG/Canvas rendering, no backend required. Build time: 2–3 weeks for a solo frontend developer. Mode 3 and classroom leaderboard are v1.1.

**Why now:** No existing tool combines angle visualisation with an estimation practice game. Online angle drawers exist but are plain and static — no classification, no comparisons, no game. The estimation game is the key differentiator: it builds intuition through repetition and immediate feedback, which is exactly what teachers want and worksheets cannot provide. Primary teacher communities on Facebook and TES are enormous and highly active — a well-made free tool spreads fast in these networks.

**Risks:**
- Differentiation is moderate — each individual feature is simple. Execution quality (smooth interaction, delightful design, smart real-world comparisons) is what separates this from a forgettable angle drawer.
- The estimation game needs careful difficulty tuning — too easy bores older kids, too hard frustrates younger ones. An adaptive difficulty curve or age-band selector may be needed quickly.
- Monetisation depends on the classroom leaderboard hook. If teachers don't convert, it stays a free tool with no revenue path.

**Recommended next step:** Product director to decide: (1) go/no-go on this as the next build, and (2) whether the estimation game alone is a sufficient MVP or whether draw-and-classify must ship simultaneously. If go, assign to design for a one-page wireframe before engineering begins.
