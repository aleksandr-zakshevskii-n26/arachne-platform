## Opportunity: Prime Factorisation Tree Builder

**Target user:** Children aged 9–12 (and their parents) working through prime factorisation homework, typically during US Common Core Grade 4–6 units, UK Key Stage 2, and equivalents worldwide.

**Problem:** Factor trees are a standard curriculum topic, but children struggle with two things: (1) the mechanical process — they forget to keep splitting until every leaf is prime, and (2) the deeper concept — that no matter which pair of factors you start with, you always arrive at the same set of primes (the fundamental theorem of arithmetic). Textbooks show one static tree. Teachers draw one example on the board. The child never sees the "same primes, different path" insight because nobody has time to draw three trees for every number.

**Our angle:** No existing free tool animates the tree construction step-by-step with plain-language explanations at each node ("12 is not prime — pick two numbers that multiply to 12"). More importantly, no tool lets the child tap "try another split" to watch 12 → 2 × 6 rebuild as 12 → 3 × 4 and see that both trees end with {2, 2, 3}. This makes the uniqueness of prime factorisation visible and discoverable rather than memorised. Existing competitors (CalculatorSoup, Math Warehouse, Mathway) produce static images or paywalled text output.

**MVP scope:** Single number input (2–9,999). Animated SVG tree that grows node-by-node with a brief text explanation per step. Primes highlighted in colour at the leaves. "Try another split" button that rebuilds the tree from a different valid factor pair. Final summary: "The prime factors of 60 are 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 — no matter how you split it." Optional print/export for homework submission. No accounts, no curriculum sequencing, no gamification. One page, one tool. Buildable MVP in 1–2 weeks.

**Why now:** The school year is in session across both hemispheres at overlapping points; factor tree units recur annually with a fresh cohort. Search traffic for "factor tree calculator" and "prime factorization" is steady and underserved by interactive tools. The build cost is minimal — this can ship before the current homework cycle ends.

**Risks:** Demand is bursty (concentrated in the weeks each class covers this unit, not year-round). The concept is narrower than fractions, limiting lifetime engagement per user. If we gate poorly, the free version may satisfy everyone. The tool could feel "too simple" — though for this platform, simplicity is a feature.

**Recommended next step:** Product director to make a go/no-go decision on building this as the next atomic tool. If go: confirm print/export as the freemium gate and define the target launch date relative to the current school calendar.
