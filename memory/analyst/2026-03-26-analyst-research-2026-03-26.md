## Opportunity: Mirror Symmetry Grid

**Target user:** Children aged 5–11 (and their teachers/parents), primarily during primary school geometry units on line symmetry (UK Year 3–5, US Grade 2–5). Secondary audience: younger children (5–6) using it as a creative pattern-making toy before formal maths instruction.

**Problem:** Symmetry is one of the first geometry concepts children encounter, but existing tools either lock kids into completing a predetermined shape (Topmarks) or bury the concept inside heavyweight platforms with accounts and gamification layers (ABCya, Mathigon). There is no standalone, zero-friction, open-ended sandbox where a child clicks cells on one side of a line and instantly sees the mirror appear on the other side. The "aha" moment — that every point has a reflected twin at equal distance from the line — gets lost when the tool is doing too much.

**Our angle:** A single-page interactive grid (6×6 or 8×8) with a visible vertical centre line. The child clicks or taps any cell on either side; the mirrored cell on the opposite side lights up simultaneously in the same colour. No typing. No instructions needed. The mirror effect is self-evident within 2 seconds of interaction. Because it is a creative sandbox (not a quiz), children replay it voluntarily — they make butterflies, faces, abstract patterns. Teachers can project it on a whiteboard and invite kids to predict where the mirror cell will appear before clicking.

**MVP scope:** Single `index.html` file, no backend, no libraries. Core logic: a CSS grid of clickable `<div>` elements. Click handler toggles `cells[row][col]` and `cells[row][gridWidth - 1 - col]`. Centre line rendered as a styled border. Estimated build time: **5–7 minutes**. Optional polish (colour picker, clear button, grid size toggle) adds 3–5 minutes but is not required for the core experience.

**Why now:** Primary teachers are actively seeking lightweight, no-login interactive tools they can project in class or share as links for homework. Post-pandemic, schools have settled into hybrid digital/physical instruction but are overwhelmed by platform fatigue. A single URL that does one thing perfectly is exactly what they want. No competitor currently offers this specific format.

**Risks:** (1) The tool may feel "too simple" — a child might exhaust it in 3 minutes. Mitigation: add a horizontal symmetry toggle and a colour palette to extend engagement. (2) Symmetry is a narrow curriculum topic; standalone traffic may be low without a bundle of similar tools. (3) The concept is trivially copyable — no defensible moat beyond being first and cleanest.

**Recommended next step:** Product director to decide go/no-go. If go, assign to a developer to build the single-file MVP and share a live URL for user testing with 2–3 primary teachers within one week.
