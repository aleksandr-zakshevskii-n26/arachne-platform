## Opportunity: Mean / Median / Mode Visual Calculator

**Target user:** Children aged 10–14 and their teachers, specifically during statistics units in Grades 5–8. Secondary audience: homeschooling parents teaching data literacy.

**Problem:** Mean, median, and mode are the first statistics concepts kids encounter, and they routinely confuse them. Textbooks define all three on the same page but never show them *together* on a single visual. Kids memorise formulas ("add and divide") without understanding *what the number represents*. The critical intuition — that the mean is a balance point, the median is a positional middle, and the mode is a frequency peak — is invisible in a list of numbers. Teachers want a live demo tool; the best they have is a static whiteboard drawing or a spreadsheet.

**Our angle:** No existing kid-facing tool shows all three measures simultaneously on an interactive dot plot. Calculator.net and Omnicalculator give numeric answers only. Khan Academy explains in video but offers no sandbox. GeoGebra is powerful but intimidating for a 10-year-old. Our tool lets a child type a set of numbers (e.g., `2, 3, 3, 7, 15`), instantly see dots on a number line with colour-coded markers for mean, median, and mode, then **add or remove numbers and watch the markers move in real time**. The "add an outlier" interaction — type `99`, watch the mean rocket right while median barely moves — is an "aha" moment that no existing tool delivers in a kid-friendly wrapper.

**MVP scope:** Single-page web app. Input: comma-separated numbers (up to 20 values, range 0–100). Output: animated dot plot on a number line with three labelled, colour-coded markers. One-sentence plain-English explanation below each marker ("The mean is 6 because all the numbers balance at this point"). Real-time update on every input change. No accounts, no backend. Estimated build: 2–3 weeks for a solo developer.

**Why now:** Data literacy is being pushed into earlier grades across US Common Core, UK National Curriculum, and IB PYP. Statistics is no longer optional in upper primary. Teacher demand for interactive demo tools is growing — evidenced by the popularity of statistics manipulatives on Teachers Pay Teachers. The tool is trivially distributable via TpT, teacher Facebook groups, and SEO for "mean median mode for kids."

**Risks:** (1) The concept is simple enough that a free tool may fully satisfy users, making premium conversion hard. Mitigation: classroom features (shareable links, challenge sets) as paid tier. (2) A competitor could replicate this in days. Mitigation: speed to market and teacher community trust matter more than defensibility at this scale. (3) Seasonal demand — statistics units cluster in specific school terms.

**Recommended next step:** Product Director to decide go/no-go on MVP build. If go, the first decision is distribution channel: standalone web app with SEO play, or Teachers Pay Teachers listing, or both. Designer should prototype the dot-plot interaction before any code is written — the animation quality *is* the product.
